Respect what you had to do to survive.


Many survivors of sexual violence blame themselves for the way they have dealt with it. You may not want to admit that you could only do so to survive. It may be hard to admit that you have to pay for something in order to greet each day. As a child at the time, you did the best you could under terrible circumstances and are still doing your best. The important thing is that you survived. It is therefore important to respect the resources you have.

Everybody's dealing with it.


We all use strategies to cope with situations where we are unable to cope, struggling or stressed out. Most of the strategies mentioned in this chapter are those used once or several times by those who struggled against difficult situations in a broad sense. There are universal ones, and some belong to certain survivors and may or may not apply to you.

Some of your ways of coping would have been nurtured with positive power (e.g., becoming a competent society, being self-fulfilling, developing a sense of humor, or overcoming a crisis), and others would have self-defeating (e.g., larceny, drug or alcoholism). Some behaviors have both positive and destructive sides. Healing is possible when you distinguish between these two. By doing so, you can respect positive power while changing the unhelpful type.

Negative


Denial is not what happens, pretending that what happened didn't happen and turning a blind eye. This is a common method found in families with alcoholics. It is also almost universal in places where kinship sexual violence occurs. "If you ignore it for long enough, it will disappear."

However, the woman replied in a trivial way. "Really? But it wasn't me. My dad doesn't hit me. Some of the survivors of sexual violence admit that they were damaged, but deny that there were any traces of the damage. One woman says, "I told my therapist I had already overcome it, and he believed me."
